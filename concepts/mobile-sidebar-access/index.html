Fixing the two-hamburger header on mobile

Six ways to give phone users access to the site nav and the Knowledge Base sidebar without two identical ☰ icons flanking the logo. Every mockup is interactive — tap the controls to feel the mechanic. Concepts 1 and 2 are your ideas; 3–6 are additional directions.

The problem: today an article page shows two identical hamburgers. Left = site nav (Home · Knowledge Base · Tools · Learn · Study · Search). Right = the rich sidebar drawer (browse-everything). Same icon, different menus, no way to tell them apart — and the sidebar is nearly a superset of the nav.

The sidebar stops being a hidden second menu and becomes an obvious, labeled “Browse” shelf. One real hamburger (site nav) stays top-left; the sidebar is text, not a twin icon.

Strengths

  • Kills the twin-icon confusion — the sidebar is now words
  • Discoverable: users see “Browse” without guessing
  • Zero overlay; content just pushes down

Trade-offs

  • Expanded list pushes the article down the page
  • Long sidebar = long accordion; may need section collapse
  • Still two separate controls (nav ☰ + Browse bar)

A persistent edge handle is a constant, discoverable affordance — like a bookmark tab poking out. It maps perfectly to “pull the sidebar out,” and the header carries only one hamburger (site nav).

Strengths

  • Always-visible cue; no guessing what a 2nd ☰ does
  • Feels physical — labeled “Browse” tab
  • Header stays clean; brand can center

Trade-offs

  • Floating tab overlaps content edge; can catch scroll
  • Left-edge swipe conflicts with iOS back-gesture
  • Some find persistent tabs visually noisy

The two menus were ~90% the same surface. This merges them: brand left, a single ☰ top-right, one drawer that starts with the top-nav links then flows into the browse sections. Nothing to disambiguate because there’s only one control.

Strengths

  • Zero ambiguity — one button, one place to go
  • Standard, boring-in-a-good-way mobile pattern
  • Cleanest header; consistent site-wide

Trade-offs

  • One long drawer; needs tidy grouping
  • Loses the “sidebar as a distinct thing” identity
  • Most code churn in Layout + Sidebar

One trigger, but the sheet rises from the bottom (thumb zone) and splits the two purposes into an explicit Navigate / Browse toggle. Keeps both menus’ full contents, names them, and is very reachable one-handed.

Strengths

  • Both menus preserved but clearly labeled
  • Bottom sheet = best thumb ergonomics
  • Modern, app-native feel

Trade-offs

  • Two taps to reach “Browse” (open, then switch)
  • Segmented control adds a small learning beat
  • More markup/JS than the disclosure options

Differentiate by words + distinct icons, not twin ☰. The header ☰ is unambiguously “site menu”; a thin sticky bar exposes the two page-scoped surfaces — the page Contents (TOC) and Browse (sidebar) — each self-labeling.

Strengths

  • Every control is labeled — no icon guessing
  • Surfaces the page TOC too (great for long articles)
  • Low risk; additive, doesn’t restructure nav

Trade-offs

  • Eats a strip of vertical space below the header
  • Three controls total (☰ + 2 pills) = busiest option
  • Two panels to manage open/close state

Move navigation out of the header entirely. A fixed bottom bar puts Menu, Contents, Search and Study in the thumb zone — the header becomes just the brand. This is the most “native app” of the set.

Strengths

  • Header fully de-cluttered; brand only
  • All key actions one thumb-tap away, always
  • Search + Study get first-class visibility

Trade-offs

  • Bottom bar eats ~56px of every screen
  • Heaviest departure from the current desktop feel
  • Can feel “appy” for a docs/reading site
